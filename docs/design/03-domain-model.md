# 03 — Domain Model

Describes the **domain model** for the Warehouse Management System (WMS).
The domain model focuses on core business concepts, how they relate to each other, and
the rules enforced by the system.

Business logic is defined in the domain model and kept separate from the user interface,
API, and database concerns. The design allows later extension if required.

---

## 1. Domain Model Overview

The domain model is split into four main areas:

1. **Suppliers and Purchasing**  managing suppliers, purchase orders, and deliveries  
2. **Inventory** tracking products, stock levels, and stock movements  
3. **Customer Orders**  processing outgoing orders and reducing stock  
4. **Finance**  recording income and expenses and generating reports  

The model follows a lightweight Domain-Driven Design (DDD) approach. It uses entities and
value objects; aggregates keep related data consistent and enforce business rules.

---

## 2. Ubiquitous Language

To avoid ambiguity, the following terms are used consistently throughout the system:

| Term | Meaning |
|-----|--------|
| Product | An item stocked and sold by the warehouse |
| Supplier | A business the warehouse purchases products from |
| Purchase Order | An order placed with a supplier to restock inventory |
| Goods Receipt | A record confirming that stock has been received |
| Stock Movement | A record of stock increasing or decreasing |
| Customer Order | A record of products sold to a customer |
| Financial Transaction | An income or expense created by a sale or purchase |
| User Role | The role selected to access system operations (Warehouse Staff, Manager, Administrator) |

---

## 3. Domain Entities

Entities have a unique identity and represent core business objects.

### Supplier
**Purpose:**  
Stores supplier details and links to purchase orders.

**Key data:**
- SupplierId
- Name
- ContactDetails

**Notes:**  
A supplier can have many purchase orders.

---

### User
**Purpose:**  
Represents the active system user and their role. Supports role-based workflows.
Authentication is out of scope.

**Key data:**
- UserId
- DisplayName
- Role (Warehouse Staff, Manager, Administrator)

**Notes:**  
Only one active user is assumed at a time (see system assumptions).

---

### Product
**Purpose:**  
Represents an item held in inventory and enforces stock rules.

**Key data:**
- ProductId
- SKU
- Name
- ReorderLevel
- UnitCost

**Key behaviour:**
- Increase stock
- Decrease stock (must not allow negative values)

---

### PurchaseOrder (Aggregate Root)
**Purpose:**  
Represents an order placed with a supplier and controls its lifecycle.

**Key data:**
- PurchaseOrderId
- SupplierId
- Status
- CreatedAt

**Key behaviour:**
- Add product lines
- Move through order statuses (Pending → Partially Received → Completed)
- Accept goods receipts
- Cancel order (Pending or Partially Received only)

A purchase order owns its order lines and is responsible for keeping them consistent.

---

### PurchaseOrderLine
**Purpose:**  
Represents a single product and quantity within a purchase order.

**Key data:**
- ProductId
- QuantityOrdered
- UnitCostAtOrder

---

### GoodsReceipt
**Purpose:**  
Provides an auditable record that goods were received for a purchase order.
Used for delivery tracking and order history requirements.

**Key data:**
- GoodsReceiptId
- PurchaseOrderId
- ReceivedAt

---

### GoodsReceiptLine
**Purpose:**  
Stores the quantity of a specific product received in a delivery.

**Key data:**
- ProductId
- QuantityReceived

---

### Customer
**Purpose:**  
Minimal customer record to support order tracking.

**Key data:**
- CustomerId
- Name

---

### CustomerOrder (Aggregate Root)
**Purpose:**  
Represents an outgoing customer order and controls order confirmation.

**Key data:**
- CustomerOrderId
- CustomerId
- Status
- CreatedAt

**Key behaviour:**
- Add order lines
- Confirm order (only if stock is available)
- Cancel order (Draft or Confirmed only)

---

### CustomerOrderLine
**Purpose:**  
Represents a product and quantity within a customer order.

**Key data:**
- ProductId
- Quantity
- UnitPriceAtSale

---

### StockMovement
**Purpose:**  
Acts as an immutable audit log of all stock changes.

**Key data:**
- StockMovementId
- ProductId
- Type (Receipt, Issue, Adjustment)
- Quantity
- OccurredAt
- Reason (for adjustments)
- ReferenceType
- ReferenceId

---

### FinancialTransaction
**Purpose:**  
Records income and expenses generated by sales and purchases.

**Key data:**
- TransactionId
- Type (Sale, PurchaseExpense, or StockAdjustment)
- Amount
- Status (Pending, Posted, Voided, Reversed)
- OccurredAt
- ReferenceType
- ReferenceId
- ReversalOfTransactionId (optional)

---

### ReportExport
**Purpose:**  
Records when financial reports are exported and in what format.

**Key data:**
- ReportExportId
- ReportType (SalesSummary, ExpenseSummary, FinancialSummary)
- Format (TXT or JSON)
- GeneratedAt
- FilePath
- DateRange (optional)

---

## 4. Value Objects

Value objects are immutable and validated.

### Money
- Amount (decimal)
- Currency (fixed to GBP)

### ContactDetails
- Email
- Phone
- Address (optional)

### DateRange
- From date
- To date

---

## 5. Aggregates and Boundaries

Aggregates keep related data consistent:

- **PurchaseOrder** is the root for purchase order lines and goods receipts  
- **CustomerOrder** is the root for customer order lines  

Only the aggregate root can be modified directly from outside the domain.

---

## 6. Key Business Rules

### Inventory
- Stock levels must never be negative
- Stock quantities must be whole numbers
- Low stock is triggered when stock is at or below the reorder level
- Stock adjustments must record a reason
- Adjustment movements may be negative to indicate decreases

### Purchasing
- Purchase orders must contain at least one line
- Ordered and received quantities must be greater than zero
- Received quantities must not exceed ordered quantities
- Purchase order status progresses through Pending → Partially Received → Completed
- Purchase orders can be cancelled only while Pending or Partially Received

### Customer Orders
- Orders must contain at least one line
- Orders cannot be confirmed if stock is insufficient
- Orders can be cancelled while Draft or Confirmed

### Finance
- All transactions must reference a purchase order or customer order
- Transaction amounts must be positive
- Currency is fixed to GBP
- Purchase orders create a Pending expense; receipts post the expense for received items
- Transactions may be voided or reversed; voided transactions are excluded from reports and reversals link to the original transaction

## 7. Repository Interfaces

Repositories abstract persistence from the domain model and allow the database
implementation to change without affecting business logic.

Planned repositories include:
- ISupplierRepository
- ICustomerRepository
- IProductRepository
- IPurchaseOrderRepository
- IGoodsReceiptRepository
- ICustomerOrderRepository
- IStockMovementRepository
- ITransactionRepository
- IReportExportRepository

## 8. Domain Diagrams and Appendix References

The domain model is supported by a set of UML diagrams that should be included in the report appendices. Each diagram explains a different view of the design: the class-level structure of the domain, the lifecycle of the main aggregates, and the interaction steps for key workflows.

### Appendix A — Domain Model Diagram
- Source: `docs/design/uml/domain-model.puml`
- Output: `docs/design/uml/domain-model.png`

This diagram provides the static view of the domain model. It shows the main entities, value objects, aggregate roots, and their relationships across the four domain areas: suppliers and purchasing, inventory, customer orders, and finance. It is the main reference for explaining how concepts such as `Product`, `PurchaseOrder`, `CustomerOrder`, `GoodsReceipt`, `StockMovement`, and `FinancialTransaction` relate to each other.

### Appendix B — Purchase Order State Diagram
- Source: `docs/design/uml/state-purchase-order.puml`

This diagram explains the lifecycle of a purchase order. It shows that a purchase order starts in `Pending`, moves to `Partially Received` when only some items have been delivered, moves to `Completed` when all ordered quantities have been received, and can be cancelled only while it is still `Pending` or `Partially Received`. This supports the rule that the aggregate controls valid status transitions.

### Appendix C — Customer Order State Diagram
- Source: `docs/design/uml/state-customer-order.puml`

This diagram explains the lifecycle of a customer order. A new order starts in `Draft`, moves to `Confirmed` when stock checks succeed, and can be cancelled while still `Draft` or after it has been `Confirmed`. The diagram therefore supports the rule that order confirmation and cancellation are controlled through the aggregate rather than by direct database updates.

### Appendix D — Sequence Diagram: Create Customer Order
- Source: `docs/design/uml/sequence-create-customer-order.puml`

This diagram explains how the `Create Customer Order` use case is carried out across system layers. It shows the API receiving the request, the application service coordinating the workflow, the `CustomerOrder` aggregate enforcing confirmation rules, product stock being checked and reduced, stock movements being recorded, and a sale transaction being created. It is useful for showing how the domain model works in practice rather than just as a static structure.

### Appendix E — Sequence Diagram: Receive Delivery
- Source: `docs/design/uml/sequence-receive-delivery.puml`

This diagram explains the full-delivery workflow for a purchase order. It shows how a delivery request creates a `GoodsReceipt`, updates product stock, records receipt stock movements, updates the purchase order status, and posts the related purchase expense. This supports the explanation that receiving goods affects several domain concepts while still being controlled through the purchase-order workflow.

### Appendix F — Sequence Diagram: Partial Delivery / Backorder
- Source: `docs/design/uml/sequence-partial-delivery.puml`

This diagram explains the partial-delivery case, which is important because not all supplier deliveries arrive in one shipment. It shows that the purchase order remains open in the `Partially Received` state, only the received quantities are added to stock, and the financial transaction reflects the partial expense posted so far. This appendix supports the design decision to treat partial delivery as a first-class domain scenario.

### Appendix G — Sequence Diagram: Adjust Stock
- Source: `docs/design/uml/sequence-adjust-stock.puml`

This diagram explains the manual stock-adjustment workflow. It shows how warehouse staff submit an adjustment, how the relevant `Product` is updated, and how a `StockMovement` is created with a reason so that the change remains auditable. This appendix is useful when explaining how the system maintains inventory traceability outside of purchase and sales workflows.

### Appendix H — Sequence Diagram: Void / Reverse Financial Transaction
- Source: `docs/design/uml/sequence-void-reverse-transaction.puml`

This diagram explains how administrators can correct financial records. It shows the difference between voiding a transaction and reversing a posted transaction, and it makes clear that the original transaction and any reversal remain linked for audit purposes. This supports the financial tracking rules described earlier in this document.
